# Blocker Escalation Implementation Guide

## 1. Introduction

This guide provides **Project Managers (PMs)** with step-by-step instructions for implementing the **Structured Blocker Escalation Procedure** within **HAAG** projects. The procedure was successfully piloted in the **R for Evolution** project and is now recommended for adoption across all HAAG initiatives.

The guide focuses on practical actions, tools, and best practices to ensure consistent and effective implementation.

---

## 2. Objectives

By following this guide, Project Managers will be able to:

- Identify and monitor blockers effectively.
- Apply structured escalation at appropriate intervals.
- Facilitate communication between researchers and faculty.
- Maintain clear documentation of blocker resolution.
- Foster accountability and proactive problem-solving within project teams.

---

## 3. Prerequisites

Before implementing the procedure, ensure the following are in place:

- **Weekly reporting mechanism** (e.g., Slack bot or standardized template).
- **Communication channels** (Slack, Discord, or equivalent).
- **Operations log** for tracking blockers and actions.
- **Access to faculty advisors or relevant stakeholders** for escalation.
- **Project dashboard or task tracking tool** (optional but recommended).

---

## 4. Step-by-Step Implementation

### Step 1: Introduce the Procedure
- Present the escalation protocol to researchers and faculty at the start of the semester.
- Clearly define what constitutes a **blocker**.
- Explain escalation timelines (Week 1–3) and stakeholder roles.
- Emphasize the importance of **continuous blocker reporting**.

### Step 2: Establish Reporting and Communication Channels
- Ensure all researchers submit **weekly reports** on time.
- Encourage the use of **Slack or Discord** for real-time updates.
- Provide a standardized format for reporting blockers.

### Step 3: Set Up the Operations Log
- Create or update a centralized log to track:
  - Blocker description
  - Date first reported
  - Owner
  - Escalation stage
  - Actions taken
  - Resolution date

### Step 4: Conduct Weekly Reviews
- Review weekly reports and dashboards.
- Identify any newly reported or recurring blockers.
- Update the operations log accordingly.

### Step 5: Apply the Escalation Protocol

#### Week 1 – Monitor
- Document the blocker and monitor progress.
- Encourage the researcher to attempt initial resolution.

#### Week 2 – Team-Level Escalation
- Flag recurring blockers for discussion in the team meeting.
- Facilitate root cause analysis.
- Define next steps and assign an **owner**.
- Record decisions in the operations log.

#### Week 3 – Faculty/Leadership Escalation
- Escalate persistent blockers to the faculty advisor or relevant stakeholder.
- Coordinate a meeting if necessary.
- Document the agreed-upon action plan and follow up until resolution.

### Step 6: Encourage Continuous Blocker Surfacing
- Remind researchers to report blockers as soon as they arise.
- Actively monitor communication channels.
- Ask clarifying questions to prevent delays.

### Step 7: Communicate Outcomes
- Share updates on resolved blockers during team meetings.
- Highlight lessons learned to promote continuous improvement.

### Step 8: End-of-Semester Review
- Evaluate the effectiveness of the procedure.
- Gather feedback from stakeholders.
- Document insights and recommendations for future iterations.

## 6. Best Practices

- **Clarify Expectations Early:** Ensure all team members understand what constitutes a blocker.
- **Promote Psychological Safety:** Encourage open communication without fear of judgment.
- **Assign Clear Ownership:** Every escalated blocker should have a responsible owner.
- **Act Proactively:** Engage with researchers throughout the week to prevent escalation.
- **Leverage Existing Tools:** Integrate the procedure into current workflows to minimize overhead.
- **Document Consistently:** Maintain accurate records to support evaluation and knowledge transfer.

---

## 7. Common Pitfalls and Mitigation Strategies

| Pitfall | Impact | Mitigation |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Misclassification of blockers | Unnecessary escalations | Provide clear definitions and examples |
| Delayed reporting | Extended task stagnation | Encourage continuous communication |
| Lack of ownership | Slow resolution | Assign a responsible owner during escalation |
| Over-reliance on weekly reports | Missed early intervention | Monitor communication channels regularly |
| Limited faculty availability | Delayed decisions | Schedule escalation touchpoints in advance |

---

## 8. Measuring Success

Even when quantitative metrics are limited, success can be evaluated using both **qualitative and quantitative indicators**.

### Quantitative Indicators (When Available)
- Number of multi-week blockers.
- Average time to blocker resolution.
- Frequency of escalations.
- Task completion rates.

### Qualitative Indicators
- Improved clarity and accountability in reporting.
- Increased proactive communication by researchers.
- Positive stakeholder feedback.
- Smoother project execution and reduced ambiguity.

## 11. Adaptation Across HAAG Projects

While the procedure was piloted in the **R for Evolution** project, which was operating without a CLEAR advisor at the time of the pilot, it is designed to be flexible and applicable to a wide range of HAAG initiatives. Project Managers are encouraged to tailor communication channels and escalation stakeholders based on project-specific needs while maintaining the core escalation principles.
